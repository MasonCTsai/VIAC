What Does It Mean To Be Blind?

BlindnessA condition that means you are unable to see well is a condition that means that you cannot see well.


This can affect one or both eyes. Another name for blindness is visual impairment.


Did you know that when someone is “blind”, it doesn't mean that they see only pitch black?


Most people who are blind can see some light, or flashes of light, meaning that if they walk into a room, they can tell if the lights are on or off.


Still though, some people do see pitch black, but this is very rare. Being completely unable to see light is called “total blindnessWhen someone is completely unable to see light.”, also known as complete blindness.


Those who are completely blind are unable to see the difference between a bright room and a dark one.

Hands-On Activity

Try It Yourself!

This is a short, interactive lesson and experiment to help you understand the effects of visual impairment on an person's life.

Make sure to read the entire step-by-step directions before blindfolding yourself!

1

Partner Up!

First, find a partner!

2

Experience

Using a blindfold, cover one person's eyes securely, so you can't see any light. If you don't have a blindfold, a cloth will do! Make sure you know where you are, in a safe environment, before blindfolding yourself.

Despite most cases of visual impairment not completely blocking out light, this activity still uses total blindness. This is because it makes the differences between living with and without sight more obvious.

3

Focus

Without being able to see, start to focus on the senses you do have access to. What do you smell, and what do you hear?

4

What Is It?

Have a partner place a random item on a table in front of you. Have the blindfolded partner try to find and name the object, using the rest of their senses.

5

Discuss!

Finally, discuss with your partner about the differences of living in and interacting with the world around you with and without a sense of sight to guide you.


1. Did you notice how difficult it was to get around, missing such an important sense?


2. How were you able to make use of your other senses to make up for your loss of sight? Which sense did you rely the strongest on?
